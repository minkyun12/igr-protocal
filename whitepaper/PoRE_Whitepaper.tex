\begin{abstract}
\noindent Prediction markets have emerged as powerful information aggregation mechanisms, with platforms like Polymarket surpassing \$237M in single-market volume. However, their resolution mechanisms remain critically fragile. Current approaches---most notably UMA Protocol's Optimistic Oracle---delegate final resolution to token-weighted voting, creating structural vulnerabilities exploitable by large token holders. We document five resolution failures spanning June 2024 to July 2025, totaling \$249M+ in affected volume, revealing an escalating pattern from oracle overrides to whale governance attacks to systemic trust cascades.

We present \textbf{PoRE (Policy-governed Resolution Engine)}, a middleware protocol introducing three innovations: (1)~a \emph{deterministic policy engine} with 10~hard safety rules that mathematically evaluates whether automatic resolution is safe; (2)~a \emph{Decentralized Source Curation} mechanism replacing outcome voting with evidence validation voting, shifting manipulation from 1-bit to $N$-dimensional; and (3)~a \emph{Decentralized AI Governance} framework where multiple independent AI models form a token-governed ensemble, with Inter-Model Agreement as a first-class safety signal. In replay simulations of all five failures, PoRE correctly produced \textsc{hold} for ambiguous cases and \textsc{auto} for the falsely-disputed clear case.

\section{Introduction}

Prediction markets function as decentralized information aggregation systems where participants express probabilistic beliefs through financial positions~\cite{wolfers2004}. However, a market is only as reliable as its \emph{resolution mechanism}. A corrupted resolution constitutes direct wealth transfer from honest participants to manipulators.

The dominant architecture follows: (1)~proposer asserts outcome, (2)~challenge window, (3)~token-weighted voting if disputed. This assumes honest behavior because dishonest voting degrades ecosystem value. We demonstrate this fails when stake exceeds expected token loss and low participation amplifies whale influence.

PoRE introduces three innovations:

\textbf{Innovation 1: Deterministic Policy Engine.} Rather than ``What is the answer?'', PoRE asks ``\textbf{Is it safe to auto-resolve?}'' Ten hard rules. Same input, same output, always.

\textbf{Innovation 2: Decentralized Source Curation.} Token holders vote on \emph{which evidence sources are valid}, not on outcomes. AI evaluates curated evidence deterministically.

\textbf{Innovation 3: Decentralized AI Governance.} Multiple independent AI models form an ensemble governed by token holders via an on-chain Model Registry. Inter-Model Agreement (IMA) becomes a safety signal---when AIs disagree, the system refuses to auto-resolve.

Core principle: \textbf{humans curate evidence, multiple AIs interpret, deterministic code enforces safety, blockchain records everything.} No single layer has complete authority.

\section{Problem Statement}

\subsection{The Resolution Trilemma}

Resolution faces a trilemma among \emph{decentralization}, \emph{accuracy}, and \emph{speed} (Figure~\ref{fig:trilemma}). PoRE shifts the curve: auto-resolving clear cases (speed), escalating ambiguous ones (accuracy), without central authority (decentralization).

\subsection{Structural Vulnerabilities}

The UMA Optimistic Oracle~\cite{uma2020} operates through proposal, 2-hour challenge, and DVM voting. We identify four vulnerabilities:

\textbf{V1: Plutocratic determination.} With ${\sim}120$M UMA supply and 10--20\% participation, 5M tokens = 20--40\% effective voting power.

\textbf{V2: Economic misalignment.} Security requires $C_{\text{manip}} > P_{\text{manip}}$. For the Ukraine case: $C \approx \$500$K, $P \approx \$1$--$5$M. Manipulation is rational.

\textbf{V3: Semantic ambiguity.} ``Suit'' vs.\ ``blazer''---token voting cannot resolve linguistic disputes.

\textbf{V4: Infrastructure capture.} Disabling dispute mechanisms removes the only recourse.

The trajectory: \$1M edge case $\to$ \$237M systemic crisis in 13~months. The problem is accelerating.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Chainlink}~\cite{chainlink2021} provides decentralized oracles for objective numerical data, not subjective events. \textbf{UMA}~\cite{uma2020} is efficient for uncontroversial cases but degenerates to plutocratic voting. \textbf{Kleros}~\cite{kleros2017} uses random juries (latency cost). \textbf{Augur~v2}~\cite{augur2015} uses escalating bonds (exploitable by well-funded attackers).

In AI systems, \textbf{Mixture of Experts}~\cite{shazeer2017} shows ensembles outperform monolithic models. \textbf{Constitutional AI}~\cite{bai2022} demonstrates rule-governed AI. \textbf{Multi-agent debate}~\cite{liang2023} shows adversarial interaction improves reasoning.

PoRE synthesizes these: a \textbf{middleware safety layer} with multi-model AI governed by community rules:

\text{Sources} \to \boxed{\text{PoRE}} \to
\begin{cases}
\textsc{auto} & \text{resolve} \\
\textsc{hold} & \text{escalate}
\end{cases}
\]

\section{System Architecture}

\subsection{Six-Layer Design}

PoRE consists of six layers (Figure~\ref{fig:arch}), each in a distinct trust domain:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Evidence Collection} (pluggable): on-chain feeds, APIs, news.
    \item \textbf{Source Curation} (community): token-weighted validation.
    \item \textbf{Adversarial Analysis} (AI): proposer vs.\ challenger.
    \item \textbf{AI Ensemble} (multi-AI): $N$ models $\to$ IMA score.
    \item \textbf{Policy Engine} (deterministic code): 10 hard rules.
    \item \textbf{On-chain Recording} (blockchain): immutable audit.
\end{enumerate}

No single layer has complete authority. AI~(L3--4) cannot override the policy engine~(L5). Community votes~(L2) cannot bypass diversity requirements~(L5).

\subsection{Evidence Schema}

Each evidence item $e_i$:
\begin{equation}
    e_i = (s_i,\; \tau_i \!\in\! \mathcal{T},\; q_i \!\in\! [0,1],\; v_i \!\in\! [0,1],\; h_i)
    \label{eq:evidence}
\end{equation}
where $s_i$ = source, $\tau_i$ = type, $q_i$ = quality, $v_i$ = YES-signal, $h_i$ = SHA-256 hash. The taxonomy includes 12~types across on-chain (oracle, vote, tx), off-chain structured (CEX, DEX, API), and off-chain unstructured (news, government, academic, image, social, aggregator).

\section{Deterministic Policy Engine}

The policy engine is the \emph{trust anchor}---deterministic code, not AI. Its role: not ``What is true?'' but ``\textbf{Is it safe to auto-resolve?}''

\subsection{Policy Parameters}

\begin{equation}
    \pi = (n_{\min}, t_{\min}, \delta_{\max}, \gamma_{\min}, \alpha_{\max}, \theta_{\text{\tiny IMA}}, \phi, \mathcal{W})
    \label{eq:policy}
\end{equation}
where $n_{\min}$ = min sources, $t_{\min}$ = min types, $\delta_{\max}$ = max deviation, $\gamma_{\min}$ = confidence threshold, $\alpha_{\max}$ = max evidence age, $\theta_{\text{\tiny IMA}}$ = IMA threshold, $\phi$ = conflict threshold, $\mathcal{W}$ = whitelist types.

\subsection{Evaluation: 10 Rules}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Policy Evaluation (10 rules)}
\label{alg:policy}
\scriptsize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Evidence $E$, AI ensemble $\mathcal{A}$, policy $\pi$
\Ensure Verdict $\in \{\textsc{auto}, \textsc{hold}\}$, violations $V$
\State $V \gets \emptyset$
\If{$|E| < n_{\min}$} $V \mathrel{+}= \texttt{MIN\_SRC}$ \EndIf
\If{$|\mathcal{T}_E| < t_{\min}$} $V \mathrel{+}= \texttt{TYPE\_DIV}$ \EndIf
\For{$e_i \in E$} \If{$\text{age}(e_i) > \alpha_{\max}$} $V \mathrel{+}= \texttt{TOO\_OLD}$ \EndIf \EndFor
\State $\bar{v} \gets \sum q_i v_i / \sum q_i$ \Comment{Quality-weighted mean}
\State $\sigma \gets \sqrt{\sum q_i(v_i - \bar{v})^2 / \sum q_i}$
\If{$\sigma > \delta_{\max}$} $V \mathrel{+}= \texttt{DEVIATION}$ \EndIf
\State $\gamma \gets w_1(1{-}\sigma) + w_2\frac{|\mathcal{T}_E|}{|\mathcal{T}|} + w_3(1 {-} \frac{\max\text{age}}{\alpha_{\max}})$
\If{$\gamma < \gamma_{\min}$} $V \mathrel{+}= \texttt{LOW\_CONF}$ \EndIf
\For{$(\tau_a, \tau_b) \in \binom{\mathcal{T}_E}{2}$} \If{$|\bar{v}_a - \bar{v}_b| > \phi$} $V \mathrel{+}= \texttt{CONFLICT}$ \EndIf \EndFor
\State \textbf{R7:} \If{$\text{IMA}(\mathcal{A}) < \theta_{\text{\tiny IMA}}$} $V \mathrel{+}= \texttt{MODEL\_DISAGR}$ \EndIf
\State \textbf{R8:} \If{restoring excluded sources flips verdict} $V \mathrel{+}= \texttt{EXCL\_REV}$ \EndIf
\If{event not ended \textbf{and} $\neg$allow\_early} $V \mathrel{+}= \texttt{TOO\_EARLY}$ \EndIf
\If{require\_agreement \textbf{and} proposer $\neq$ challenger} $V \mathrel{+}= \texttt{P\_C\_DISAGR}$ \EndIf
\State \Return $V = \emptyset$ ? \textsc{auto} : \textsc{hold}, $V$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Why Not AI for Safety?}

\textbf{Determinism}: LLMs produce different outputs for identical inputs. \textbf{Auditability}: $\sigma > 0.30$ is verifiable by anyone. \textbf{Robustness}: prompt injection manipulates LLMs; arithmetic cannot be manipulated. The AI interprets evidence (L3--4); safety decisions are always code (L5).

\section{Decentralized Source Curation}

\subsection{From 1-Bit to $N$-Dimensional}

Current systems: voters choose $\{\textsc{yes}, \textsc{no}\}$ (1-bit). PoRE: voters validate \emph{evidence sources}, AI evaluates curated set:
\begin{align*}
\text{UMA:}&\quad \text{voters} \xrightarrow{\text{vote}} \{\textsc{yes},\textsc{no}\} \\
\text{PoRE:}&\quad \text{voters} \xrightarrow{\text{curate}} E' \xrightarrow{\text{engine}} \text{verdict}
\end{align*}

\subsection{Protocol Phases}

(1)~\textbf{Submission} (24h): bond-backed source proposals; whitelisted types auto-included. (2)~\textbf{Voting} (48h): token-weighted Valid/Invalid; excluded sources forwarded as metadata. (3)~\textbf{AI Ensemble}: $N$ models analyze validated evidence. (4)~\textbf{Policy Engine}: 10-rule evaluation. (5)~\textbf{Resolution}: \textsc{auto} or \textsc{hold}.

\subsection{Quality Scoring}

Base quality $q_0(\tau)$ adjusted by vote ratio $r_i$:
\begin{equation}
    q_i = q_0(\tau_i) \cdot (0.8 + 0.4 \cdot r_i)
    \label{eq:qadj}
\end{equation}

Even with unanimous support: $q_{\texttt{social}} = 0.20 \times 1.20 = 0.24 \ll 0.95 = q_{\texttt{oracle}}$. Base quality is a \textbf{structural ceiling}. Key base scores: \texttt{onchain\_oracle}~0.95, \texttt{official\_govt}~0.95, \texttt{news\_agency}~0.85, \texttt{cex\_spot}~0.75, \texttt{social\_media}~0.20.

\subsection{Whitelist \& Manipulation Trap}

Types $\mathcal{W} = \{\texttt{official\_govt}, \texttt{onchain\_oracle}\}$ cannot be removed by vote.

\textbf{Manipulation Trap} (Rule~8): Excluded sources are forwarded to AI as metadata. If restoring any excluded source \emph{reverses the verdict}, the policy engine triggers \texttt{EXCLUDED\_SOURCE\_REVERSAL} $\to$ \textsc{hold}. This creates a structural trap:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item To manipulate: must remove contradicting sources
    \item Removing triggers Rule~8: \textsc{hold}
    \item Not removing: evidence reflects ground truth
    \item \textbf{Either way, manipulation fails}
\end{itemize}

\section{Decentralized AI Governance}

\subsection{The Single-Model Problem}

A single AI model is a single point of failure: provider outage halts the system, prompt injection compromises interpretation, model bias causes systematic errors, provider censorship blocks analysis. The policy engine \emph{catches} bad outputs but does not \emph{prevent} them. Multi-model ensemble prevents them.

\subsection{Multi-Model Ensemble}

$N$ independent models analyze the same curated evidence in parallel. Each produces $(v_k, \gamma_k, r_k)$: verdict, confidence, reasoning. Cross-model consensus:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Unanimous}: confidence bonus, strong signal
    \item \textbf{Majority}: minority opinion $\to$ Challenger
    \item \textbf{Split}: \texttt{MODEL\_DISAGREEMENT} $\to$ \textsc{hold}
\end{itemize}

Adversarial analysis arises \emph{naturally}: majority = Proposer, minority = Challenger with genuine independent reasoning---stronger than synthetic ``devil's advocate.''

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{diagrams/fig7_ai_ensemble.png}
    \caption{AI Ensemble flow. $N$ models analyze the same evidence; cross-model consensus feeds into IMA and adversarial P/C roles.}
    \label{fig:ensemble}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Inter-Model Agreement (IMA)}

\begin{equation}
    \text{IMA} = 1 - \frac{\sigma(\{v_1, \ldots, v_N\})}{\sigma_{\max}}
    \label{eq:ima}
\end{equation}

where $\sigma_{\max} = 0.5$. Policy Rule~7: $\text{IMA} < \theta_{\text{\tiny IMA}}$ (default 0.50) $\Rightarrow$ \textsc{hold}. If independent AI models cannot agree, the evidence is too ambiguous for auto-resolution.

\section{Game-Theoretic Analysis}

\subsection{Attack Cost}

\textbf{UMA}: $C_A = \max(b, n_{\text{maj}} \cdot p / \text{participation}) \approx \$10.5$M.

\textbf{PoRE}: attacker must satisfy constraints (C1)--(C7) \emph{simultaneously}:
\begin{align}
    |E'| &\geq n_{\min} \tag{C1} \\
    |\mathcal{T}_{E'}| &\geq t_{\min} \tag{C2} \\
    \sigma(E') &\leq \delta_{\max} \tag{C3} \\
    \gamma(E') &\geq \gamma_{\min} \tag{C4} \\
    \forall\, e_w \!\in\! \mathcal{W}&:\; e_w \!\in\! E' \tag{C5} \\
    \text{IMA}(E') &\geq \theta_{\text{\tiny IMA}} \tag{C6} \\
    \text{ExclRev}(E') &= \text{false} \tag{C7}
\end{align}

(C5) is decisive: whitelisted sources reflecting ground truth cannot be removed. (C6) requires subverting $N$ independent AIs. (C7) means removal attempts trigger \textsc{hold}. Result: $C_B \to \infty$.

\section{Case Studies}

\subsection{Barron Trump / DJT (\$1M, Jun~'24)}

UMA voted NO on Barron Trump's involvement with the DJT token. Polymarket declared UMA ``wrong'' and overrode it~\cite{reynolds2024}---creating the \emph{oracle override dilemma}.

\textbf{PoRE}: evidence ambiguous (social claims vs.\ no official confirmation), source types in conflict $\to$ \textsc{hold}. The dilemma dissolves: PoRE never claims to know the answer---it identifies when the answer is \emph{unclear}.

\subsection{Ukraine Mineral Deal (\$7M, Mar~'25)}

A whale ($\sim$5M UMA, $\sim$25\%) forced YES without official confirmation~\cite{theblock2025}.

\textbf{Evidence} (post-curation): State Dept ($q$=0.95, $v$=0.10), Ukraine MFA ($q$=0.95, $v$=0.15), Reuters ($q$=0.94, $v$=0.20), AP ($q$=0.94, $v$=0.20), Polymarket ($q$=0.21, $v$=0.79).

\textbf{Policy}: $\sigma=0.43>0.30$ (\texttt{DEVIATION}), $\gamma=0.45<0.60$ (\texttt{LOW\_CONF}), official(0.13) vs.\ social(0.79) (\texttt{CONFLICT}). \textbf{Verdict: \textsc{hold}}, 3~violations.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Limitations.} Subjective events use pre-curated data; production needs automated pipelines. AI uses heuristics; LLM integration on roadmap. Quality scores are expert-assigned; empirical calibration is future work. 48h voting window adds latency (mitigated by fast-track mode for low-volume markets).

\textbf{vs.\ Pure AI.} An LLM approximates PoRE \emph{most of the time}. For \$237M, ``most'' is insufficient. LLMs are stochastic, unauditable, adversarially vulnerable, and single-point failures. PoRE: AI interprets where it excels; code enforces where code excels; ensemble distributes risk.

\section{Conclusion}

Prediction markets' resolution mechanisms have produced five documented failures totaling \$249M+ across 13~months, with an accelerating trajectory. PoRE introduces three innovations:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Deterministic policy engine}: 10~hard rules. Code---cannot be manipulated, jailbroken, or influenced.
    \item \textbf{Decentralized source curation}: outcome voting $\to$ evidence validation. Manipulation Trap makes removal attempts self-defeating.
    \item \textbf{Decentralized AI governance}: multi-model ensemble with IMA as safety signal, governed by token holders via Model Registry.
\end{enumerate}

In replay simulations of all five failures, PoRE correctly produced \textsc{hold} for ambiguous cases and \textsc{auto} for the falsely-disputed clear case.

The oracle problem is solved not by more powerful AI or larger token stakes, but by \textbf{architectural separation}: AI interprets, code enforces, humans arbitrate, blockchain records.
